Friedrich Nietzsche was a German philosopher known for his radical ideas about morality, power, and the human will. He famously declared that "God is dead," arguing that traditional values were losing their meaning in modern society. His concepts of the Übermensch (Superman) and eternal recurrence challenge individuals to create their own purpose and live boldly. His work has influenced existentialism, psychology, and postmodern thought.


Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel was a German philosopher who developed the idea of dialectics—where progress happens through contradictions being resolved. His philosophy emphasized that history unfolds through a process of thesis, antithesis, and synthesis. Hegel also saw reality as deeply connected, believing that everything is part of an evolving whole. His ideas shaped modern political theory, existentialism, and even Marxism.


Albert Camus was a French philosopher and writer known for his philosophy of absurdism. He believed that life has no inherent meaning, but instead of despairing, we should embrace the absurd and live with passion. His famous works, such as The Stranger and The Myth of Sisyphus, explore themes of existential crisis and personal freedom. Camus rejected nihilism and instead encouraged finding joy in the simple act of living.
